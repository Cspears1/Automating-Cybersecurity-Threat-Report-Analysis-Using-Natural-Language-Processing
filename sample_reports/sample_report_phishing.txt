Several employees in the finance and human resources departments received an email that appeared to come from the company benefits office. The message stated that payroll and direct deposit records had to be verified before the end of the business day because of a system migration. The email used a professional tone and included the employee’s department name, which made the message appear more trustworthy. The message directed users to employee-benefits-review.com and asked them to enter their company username, password, and multi-factor authentication code to confirm their account status.

Security analysts reviewed mail logs and discovered that the sender address did not match the company’s normal benefits notification domain. One employee clicked the link and outbound traffic was later observed from the workstation to 172.64.88.21. The attached document referenced CVE-2024-3094 to make the message seem technical, but the vulnerability reference did not match the payroll topic. Analysts also identified a suspicious attachment hash of e99a18c428cb38d5f260853678922e03.

The security team should block the domain, monitor traffic to the IP address, reset any affected user credentials, and review authentication logs for unusual sign-in attempts. Employees should also be warned about fake payroll verification requests, especially messages that pressure users to act quickly or enter login information outside of approved company systems.
